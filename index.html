Ardian and Verne Announce Digital Infrastructure Hub in Ile-de-France to Support European Industrial Capabilities at Choose France

Reference: Ardian press release

Background and Summary

Ardian and Verne announced a major digital infrastructure hub in Ile-de-France, including a proposed AI gigafactory and data centre campus with a planned connection capacity of up to 500 MW. The initial phase is expected to reach approximately 200+ MW by 2030, with the overall digital infrastructure investment estimated at up to EUR 5 billion.

In parallel, Ardian plans to invest up to EUR 3 billion in renewable energy projects, representing approximately 2.5 GW of renewable capacity by 2030. This indicates that large-scale AI infrastructure is increasingly being developed alongside dedicated low-carbon electricity procurement strategies.

Discussion

The proposed AI campus represents a high-density and potentially low-flexibility electrical demand that may materially affect transmission capacity, connection queues, local reinforcement requirements, and low-carbon procurement strategies.

From a power system perspective, the key issue is not only annual electricity consumption, but also the size, location, timing, and firmness of demand. A 500 MW data centre connection should therefore be assessed as a strategic industrial load rather than a conventional commercial electricity customer.

Technical Framing

The proposed 500 MW connection capacity is equivalent to a strategic industrial load and should be assessed against regional transmission headroom, firm capacity availability, reinforcement lead times, and system carbon intensity.

P_data centre(t) = P_IT(t) + P_cooling(t) + P_aux(t)

System identification can be used to learn a mathematical model of the data centre from measured input-output data. Suitable methods may include regression, ARIMA models, Gaussian processes, and neural networks.

Control Objective

Minimise: Electricity cost + Carbon emission cost + Load fluctuation

The data centre operator should optimise grid import, battery dispatch, cooling strategy, and workload scheduling while maintaining service reliability.

Technical Constraints and Limitations

Connection capacity

Grid import must not exceed the agreed connection capacity.

Critical supply continuity

Critical IT loads must be supplied continuously to maintain compute reliability.

Battery operation

Battery state of charge should be managed to ensure safety, asset lifetime, and emergency backup capability.

Network hosting capacity

Transmission capacity, available headroom, regional constraints, distribution reinforcement, transformers, high-voltage networks, and fault level studies should be assessed.

Converter-rich load

Voltage stability should be evaluated due to UPS systems, rectifiers, power supplies, and other converter-based equipment.

Peak coincidence

Peak demand coincidence should be assessed against regional or national system peak periods.

Recommendations

Flexible demand services

AI data centres should be designed to provide flexible demand services without compromising compute reliability. Non-critical computing workloads, battery systems, and cooling flexibility may reduce grid stress during constrained periods.

HVDC and low-carbon power transfer

HVDC links may support the spatial mismatch between low-carbon generation zones and concentrated data centre demand clusters.

Economic and Planning Considerations

Grid reinforcement and locational pricing

Data centres should be assessed under cost-reflective grid charging principles. Where a large data centre creates substantial reinforcement requirements, cost allocation and locational pricing should be reviewed.

Renewable procurement and carbon matching

Annual renewable procurement alone does not guarantee low-carbon electricity in every operating hour. Carbon performance should use time-varying grid carbon intensity.

Cost and carbon control measures

Battery control, cooling pre-load, and workload scheduling can shift flexible demand toward periods with lower electricity prices, lower carbon intensity, or greater network capacity.

Conclusion

Large AI data centres should be treated as strategic electricity system assets, not passive commercial loads.

A robust assessment should include grid impact, low-carbon supply strategy, and flexibility and control options. Large-scale AI infrastructure should be integrated into power system planning, low-carbon procurement strategy, and future flexibility market design.

CEng angle

Use this case to discuss strategic load assessment, grid connection constraints, low-carbon procurement, demand flexibility, converter-rich load behaviour, and how engineering judgement links public digital infrastructure to electricity system planning.

Securing Open Data in Energy: Triage in Data Best Practice Guidance

Reference: Ofgem consultation

Executive Summary

Open energy data should be treated as a managed cyber-physical infrastructure interface, balancing openness for innovation with cybersecurity and system resilience.

Technical Risk Framework

Risk = Likelihood x Impact x Public Exposure
Public Exposure = f(Temporal Granularity, Geographical Granularity, Asset Specificity, Cross-linkability)

Conclusion

Energy network data should be opened through a risk-based triage process to support smart grid innovation without avoidable vulnerabilities.
